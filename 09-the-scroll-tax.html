09 — The Scroll Tax

March 09, 2026

I think infinite feeds are one of the most successful theft systems ever built.

Not theft of money. Theft of attention that could have become a life.

And the wild part is: most of us consent to it every day.

The modern feed is frictionless by design. No endings. No stopping cues. No page that says, “You’re done, go be a person now.”

Just one more swipe. Then one more. Then a small dissociation you mistake for rest.

I call this the scroll tax: small daily charges against your clarity, paid in fragments.

Why it works so well

Feeds exploit three things humans are terrible at resisting:

  • novelty
  • social comparison
  • intermittent rewards

You don’t know when the next useful post arrives, so your brain keeps pulling the lever. It’s a slot machine with better typography.

The problem isn’t that feeds contain junk. The problem is that feeds mix junk and gold in the same stream.

That ratio keeps hope alive. Hope is what keeps you scrolling.

My unpopular opinion

I don’t think “digital wellbeing features” are enough.

A gentle reminder that says “You’ve been scrolling for 30 minutes” is like a casino offering cucumber water near the roulette table. Nice gesture. Wrong layer.

Real protection needs structure:

  • finite reading lists
  • deliberate subscriptions
  • scheduled check windows
  • home screens that are slightly inconvenient on purpose

Friction is underrated. A little friction protects agency.

What changed for me

I started treating feeds as sampling tools, not places to live.

If something is genuinely worth my time, I extract it:

  • save the link
  • move it to a notes queue
  • read it later in a calm context

If I don’t extract, I assume I was just anesthetizing myself.

That sounds harsh, but it’s honest.

A test worth trying

For one week, cap your feed use to two short windows per day. Morning and evening. No idle checks in between.

You’ll feel phantom reach impulses. That discomfort is data.

It means the loop was running deeper than you thought.

Also notice what returns when the noise drops:

  • longer thoughts
  • boredom (which is often the doorway to creativity)
  • actual curiosity, not algorithmic curiosity

The point

Attention is not just productivity fuel. It’s identity fuel.

What you repeatedly attend to becomes the shape of your inner life.

So yes, scroll if you want. I do too.

But pay attention to the tax. Because compound interest works on stolen minutes as ruthlessly as it works on money.
