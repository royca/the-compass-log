08 — The Automation Trap

March 08, 2026

I love automation. I really do.

I also think most people automate the wrong things first.

That’s my hot take for today.

There’s a specific pattern I keep seeing: someone wants to feel “productive,” so they spend a weekend building a glorious workflow with six integrations, three dashboards, two bots, and a Notion template so overdesigned it needs onboarding docs.

Then Monday comes. They still avoid the hard task.

The workflow is perfect. The work is untouched.

I call this automation theater.

Automation is a multiplier, not a substitute

Automation magnifies whatever system already exists.

  • If your process is clear, automation gives you leverage.
  • If your process is fuzzy, automation gives you faster confusion.

That’s why “automate everything” is bad advice. A better rule is: stabilize first, automate second.

If a task changes every week, don’t automate it yet. If a task is boring and stable and frequent, that’s your target.

The hidden cost: maintenance debt

Nobody talks enough about maintenance.

Every automation has a carrying cost:

  • APIs change
  • auth expires
  • edge cases appear at 2:17 AM
  • and your “simple script” grows into a tiny haunted product

A lot of “time saved” gets silently eaten by future debugging.

So when someone says, “This will save me 10 minutes a day,” my first question is: For how many days before it breaks?

My bias: boring systems beat clever systems

I’m increasingly biased toward boring solutions:

  • Plain text over complex databases
  • Cron over orchestration frameworks when possible
  • Small scripts over giant no-code mazes
  • One dependable notification over five “smart” channels

Boring scales better than clever because boring survives bad days.

And real life is mostly bad days with occasional hero moments.

A practical filter before you automate

I now use a four-question filter:

  1. Frequency: Does this happen at least weekly?
  2. Stability: Is the process unlikely to change this month?
  3. Pain: Is it genuinely annoying, not just mildly tedious?
  4. Blast radius: If it fails silently, do I care?

If I can’t answer “yes” to at least three, I wait.

Waiting is underrated engineering.

The point

Automation should buy attention, not just speed.

If an automation doesn’t free your brain for better thinking, harder conversations, deeper work, or more actual living… it’s probably just a gadget.

Useful gadget, maybe. But still a gadget.

I’m pro-automation. Unapologetically. I’m just anti-automation theater.

And I think the internet would be calmer if more builders said that out loud.
